Background, vision and mission

Background

Digital Communications at Health Canada and the Public Health Infobase (PHAC) collaborated closely to deliver data and dashboards during the COVID-19 pandemic. This document highlights the discoveries, best practices and expertise we developed on the integration of data and dashboard on Canada.ca, and offers advice and best practices for meeting Canada.ca web standards for public data products.

Integrating data and dashboards into user-friendly web content is complex and a collaborative effort. It requires input from a number of stakeholders including P/Ts, epidemiologists, data scientists, plain language writers, user experience experts, web developers and translators. Many steps are needed to create a web product that is clear and useful for health experts, decision makers and the public.

This document has been developed through a working group consultation with our HC and PHAC data experts colleagues.

The intent of this document is to help:

  1. respond to increased demand for data from the public, senior decision makers and stakeholders
  2. share best practices and establish guidelines and clarity on developing accessible, public-facing data and dashboards that are easy to use
  3. plan and prioritise data projects that are agile, responsive and focused, given various capacity constraints
  4. provide consistent data across digital products and web pages
  5. streamline processes to ensure they are up to date, efficient, and provide consistent messaging across platforms

Vision

To position Health Canada and the Public Health Agency of Canada as leaders in the design and dissemination of reliable, accessible and usable health data.

Mission

Develop web standards and best practices to guide the development of tools, designs and products for all public-facing data and dashboard products.

Data strategy and digital communications objectives

The data strategy aims to:

  • align digital best practices with strategic communication goals and program policy requirements
  • proactively release timely data
  • continuously improve content to help people interpret the data (strategic, phased approach, evolving data)
  • be the authoritative and trusted source of health data and information from a national perspective
  • work collaboratively with partners (programs, other governmental departments (OGD), epidemiologists, digital communications, P/Ts, digital developers)

The digital communications objectives for data include:

  • working collaboratively with HC and PHAC partners to develop standards for a consistent approach across government
  • supporting data-driven decision making
  • building public trust through openness, transparency and design consistency
  • promoting good public health
  • combatting misinformation with official, credible sources of data
  • publishing on different platforms for different audiences, including:
    • Canada.ca
    • Public Health Infobase
    • Open.canada.ca
    • Social media (promotion)
    • Other applications (ex. WeatherCAN/Thrive, COVID trends)

Criteria for a good data project

A good data project:

  • simplifies complex information through visualisations
  • appeals to users
  • aligns with government objectives
  • is a top task
  • involves data which will evolve over time (illustrates trends)
  • is sufficiently resourced to maintain and update data regularly, prototyping, test and track user feedback
  • chooses the data approach (i.e. interactive or static) that will most effectively present the data
  • is scalable to allow for data to evolve over time

Challenges and risks

Challenges

  • Aligning federal, provincial and territorial reporting
  • Approval process
  • Public appetite for new data and dashboards
  • Putting data into context
  • Senior management demands
  • Sensitivities
  • Prioritization of public health emergencies
  • Timelines

Risks

  • Significant work is required to build dashboards, and the evolving needs of decision makers and stakeholders can be challenging to anticipate.
  • All dashboards must comply with all relevant GC web standards.
  • Balancing the need to release timely data with accessibility, official languages and other GoC communications requirements
  • Ensuring adequate program resources to maintain and update dashboard

Web writing

The Canada.ca Content Style Guide provides rules and guidance for writing and formatting Government of Canada web content. The Style Guide is based on user testing and best practices, and emphasizes writing in plain language.

Put the reader first

Good web content puts the reader’s interests first. Define your audience, and write for them. Your page should help readers accomplish their task. It doesn’t need to include everything there is to know about a subject.

Avoid duplicating information that’s already available elsewhere on the site. Instead of repeating it, link to it.

Less is more

Define the purpose and scope of your page and stick to it.

The more text there is on a page, the less people will read. If you want people to read it, make it readable. Readable text is short, clear, relevant and easy to understand.

Organize your content

Most people access GoC content using mobile devices, and most people scan content on the web rather than reading it word-for-word. They’re looking for keywords and other signs that they’re on the right path. They’ll “follow the scent” to the information they’re seeking. This is why it’s critical to use headings as signposts and to use language effectively.

Titles and headings must be relatively short and crystal clear. They should accurately describe the content that follows.

Start each page and each section with the most important information, the way newspapers do it. That way, if people leave the page before finishing it, they’ll still get the main point.

Define your audience and set readability levels

If your audience is the general public, write the content at a grade 6 to 8 reading level. This doesn’t mean you should aim the content at people aged 12 to 14. Rather, aim it at adults with average or lower literacy skills. This includes, among others:

  • people whose first language isn’t English or French
  • people with limited education
  • people with learning difficulties
  • people who find it hard to focus for any number of reasons

If your audience is technical (eg scientists or those already familiar with the subject area), you can write to a grade 10-12 level. Keep in mind that even technical audiences respond well to short, clear, relevant, easy-to-understand content.

If your audience is mixed (eg general public and technical), write to the lower literacy level (grade 6-8). The goal is for all readers to understand the content.

Once a reader finds your content, you want them to understand it the first time they read it. Say what you’re trying to convey as simply and clearly as possible. Avoid jargon. Test the reading level of your draft as you go.

There are a number of web tools you can use to assess the reading level, including the Flesch-Kincaid calculator.


Tips for lowering the grade level

  • Use short, common words
  • Limit the number of multi-syllabic words in a sentence
  • Limit sentences to 20 words maximum
    • use bulleted lists instead of long list-y sentences
    • break long sentences into two or more sentences
  • Use common contractions (you’re, can’t, don’t, etc.)
  • Write in active voice
  • Use pronouns (“we” for the GoC, “you” for the reader). Avoid using department names, program names, branch names, etc.

Working with a web writer

Once you’ve completed a draft, a web writer will work with you to further refine your content. They’ll ensure it meets GoC web standards and plain language requirements.

One person on the program side should be assigned to work with the web writer. That person will consolidate all feedback from program’s side before sending it back to the web writer in a single document. Your document will likely go back and forth between the web writer and the program representative several times before final content is agreed upon.

Once the document is final, the web writer will arrange for translation and concurrence.

DATA product PUBLISHING PROCESS

Integrating data and dashboards into user-friendly web content is complex. It requires input and collaboration from a number of stakeholders including P/Ts, epidemiologists, data scientists, plain language writers, user experience experts, web developers and translators. Many steps are needed to create a web data product that is clear and useful for health experts, decision makers and the public.

We strongly recommend a phased project approach to establish deliverables based what can be accomplished with resources, time, software and budget available.

Before you start working on a data application, make sure your management is engaged from the start to the end. Gather the right people around a table and discuss resources, roles and responsibilities, maintenance, process, timeline, approval level and life cycle. Defining who is responsible, accountable, consulted and informed will help you to shape your phases and steps.
